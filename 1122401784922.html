THE SHORT(/I-HAVE-FINALS-CAN'T-READ-LONG-THINGS) VERSION:


With finals fast approaching and the days growing ever shorter, many of us at Princeton share the path with the troubled protagonist of Schubert's Winterreise , searching for warmth in a harsh winter landscape. This song cycle explores the depths of despair but, in my opinion, ultimately reinvigorates the audience with a profound and ecstatic hope for the future. And it's going to be performed by opera super-star Joyce DiDonato with one of the world's most famous conductors, Yannick NÃ©zet-SÃ©guin, at the piano.

 
THE LONG (/I-HAVE-FINALS-NEED-TO-PROCRASTINATE) VERSION:
 
Winterreise is an impassioned song cycle that tells of a man who, after losing his love, sets out on a solitary journey through a bleak landscape in the depths of winter. He walks alone with his thoughts, his head downturned, seeing only footprints in the past and an icy path to the future. As he moves from town to town, little things remind him of the flame that used to burn within him. At one stop, he sees a postman coming from the town of his beloved, filling him with longing to return to kinder times. He becomes consumed by howling winds and the emptiness inside, until finally he comes upon a signpost. The path is split into two, one leading towards the next town and the other leading off into the dark forest from which no one ever returns. He takes the former, and this signals his choice to hold on to the hope that spring will return and that he will find love again. By the end of the cycle, his heart has thawed, and he sees the crisp white of the snow once again.

While it may not be as dramatized, many of us, as students at Princeton, have had similar experiences in our lives. 

With the stress of love, life, and learning all around us, it can be very difficult to hang on to the hope necessary to live our best life. Winter doesn't help with this. The frigid air and seeming despair encroach on many aspects of our life and can leave us feeling isolated-despite friends, loved ones, and colleagues being alongside us. As someone familiar with depression, anxiety, and SAD myself, I know how hard things can be, especially with the default academic strain that Princeton poses. 

I hope that by coming to this concert, those of you who struggle with these feelings can find respite in the warmth of our auditorium, and take a moment to let go, let it all wash over you, and remember that as long as you put one step in front of the other and hold onto hope, you will see brighter days.
 
Yours Truly,
Alexander de Gogorza Moravcsik '23
PUC Student Ambassador Manager
