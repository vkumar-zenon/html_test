Celebrating First Marathons in the Second City and D.C., with a little help from DRC
By Ian Pierce

Any experienced marathoner knows you never forget your first. And numerous runners who trained with DRC ran their first full marathons at two of the country's most unforgettable races, the Chicago Marathon and the Marine Corps Marathon in Washington, D.C., both this past October.

We talked to five of these first-time marathoners and asked how their DRC training helped them cross the finish line of their first official 26.2-mile distances.

In alphabetical order, they are Jona Berta, Kevin Cox, Mimi Cox, Jim Kerins and LaQuesha Scott.

Before Chicago, Jona's running experience included about a dozen half marathons since 2016. Despite doubling the race distance, she felt "totally prepared", thanks to DRC. She began with summer training to focus on hills and speed workouts, then moved into Early Marathon Training.

Thank to the Saturday long runs and the workout schedule, she said, "I knew that I could handle the distance. Additionally, the Pace Leaders also prepare you to be mentally ready for the race, too." 

Mimi and Kevin Cox, married for 37 years, are avid runners and DRC volunteers who chose Chicago for their first full marathon together. Kevin noted that the DRC training helped him accomplish his first-marathon goal not only because of its proven training plan but also "having an early marathon group with experienced runners to give advice and keep me on track. Drawing information from the experienced marathoners makes it invaluable."

Mimi explained, "The training plan definitely gives you the endurance. But the accountability and the support from the training community is what motivated me." However, her first-marathon didn't go entirely as planned. "I ran the first half strong and holding back for the second half. Then right after 13.1 miles, I helped a lady who had fallen. I twisted when I picked her up."

The move pulled a joint in her back out of place. "So the sec ond half, I was praying that I would finish. Walking was next to impossible. But I knew how good I was running, I knew that I was trained and ready. But more important, I knew I was meant to be there at that moment to help her. I had many people tracking me and giving me such support after I got hurt. So I was okay with everything." She added, smiling, "But seeing medical at the finish line was amazing."

Jim began running in 2014 when he joined the DRC Half Marathon program. "I've been with DRC ever since, except for a full year I lost to injury/surgery/rehab." Before his first marathon, Jim completed a dozen half marathons.
 
Through his experience with the Half program, Jim knew the Full program would help him reach his goal. "I had no concerns about the Full program not working. It all comes down to working the plan and making sure to listen to your body. That's the beauty of the programs at DRC - they are singular and separate, but they build on each other. Anyway, we all know that life gets in the way and the ability to run five days a week isn't always possible. The coaches know this and are willing to help runners tweak things to meet their needs."
 
LaQuesha has been an avid runner since childhood and has completed enough 13.1-mile races to earn the right to call herself a "Half Marathon Maniac." She considered training solo for her first full due to her running background but decided to train with DRC after someone suggested it.
 
"I am glad my running mate suggested it. Because of all the hard work we put into our four months of training, we were able to go to Chicago fully prepared to finish upright and strong." She added, "The most valuable aspect of the program is the support and advice from the Run Leads. Coupled with that and trusting the process of the training plan made an excellent recipe for race day success."
 
These five marathons all had different paces and different experiences, but what they shared in common was the elation of crossing the finish line and becoming Marathoners! As LaQuesha explained, " I felt accomplished, grateful and blessed to finish such a huge race and to be apart of the 1% club. Yes, I am a Marathoner."
 
Now, for each, the next decision is: Another marathon, or one-and-done? As experienced marathoners know, the best time to answer that question is after the pain recedes and the memories remain!
