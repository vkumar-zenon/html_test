NEW RESOURCE: The Critical Role of Intermediary Organizations in Expanding Youth Apprenticeship
States and communities across the country are laser-focused on expanding work-based learning opportunities for high school students in order to expose them to career options and provide more meaningful experiences outside of the classroom. A major challenge in this work is building the capacity of both the school systems and the local employer community to provide these experiences at scale.  

Last month, ESG released a report, The Critical Role of Intermediary Organizations in Expanding Youth Apprenticeship, to provide insights into the unique combination of resources, expertise and relationships required to build work-based learning systems at scale. We identified leading intermediary organizations across the country that are playing pivotal roles in expanding apprenticeships for students, revealing what they are doing to connect schools and students with employers. 

Our new report lays out the important functions work-based learning intermediaries perform and explores the how they do this work. It offers recommendations for states and local communities that want to build a stronger intermediary capacity. The report is one of a series of resources produced through the Partnership to Advance Youth Apprenticeship (PAYA), of which ESG is a member. In Spring, ESG will offer an opportunity for local communities to receive technical assistance in building and scaling an intermediary to support work-based learning. If you are interested in this opportunity, please contact Emily Passius.

Congress Finally Simplifies the FAFSA â Now Itâs Time for States to Drive Completion
Last week, Congress passed bipartisan legislation to simplify the FAFSA for the approximately 20 million families who complete it each year. The FUTURE Act accomplishes two critical objectives: it (1) permanently funds historically black colleges and universities and minority-serving institutions and (2) simplifies the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) form by removing up to 22 of the 108 questions on the form. The FAFSA simplification measures are a significant win for students and families. In addition to minimizing the burden of completing the complex form, the legislation will essentially put an end to the painfully bureaucratic verification process. 

While this action from the federal government is welcomed, it will not be sufficient to address the glaring gap in FAFSA completion rates seen nationwide. Each year, over a third of high school seniors fail to complete the FAFSA, leaving an estimated $3.4 billion in financial aid on the table. A simpler form will help, but in order to substantially increase completion rates, especially among students who are traditionally underrepresented in higher education, state and local leaders will need to put new policies in place and adopt more aggressive FAFSA completion campaigns. For instance, Michigan Governor Gretchen Whitmer recently announced a statewide FAFSA Challenge to catalyze cross-sector action to increase completion rates.

In January, Education Strategy Group will release a report, Fast Track FAFSA Completion, that will outline key strategies that have been proven to dramatically increase FAFSA completion rates in states around the country. We look forward to supporting leaders that want to make this a priority in 2020.
