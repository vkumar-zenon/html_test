CCRPI Update

At the November Curriculum and Data Team meeting, Tim Jarboe shared the latest Foothills CCRPI scores. There were many highlights and points to celebrate:

Compared to 2017, the overall Content Mastery score improved by 4 points to 47.4, although there is still work to do come up to the state average of 69.4. Particularly noteworthy was the growth in all four key content areas:

  • English Language Arts, up 2.05 points to 53.47
  • Mathematics, up 0.48 to 38.36
  • Science, up 8.36 to 42.43
  • Social Studies, up 7.24 to 55.32

The Progress Score, which measures proficiency growth in the four content areas, was down from the previous year, but still registered an impressive 84.1, two full points above the state average.

In Closing Gaps, which assesses the extent to which all students and subgroups met improvement targets, Foothills grew by 5.3 points to 91.7, nearly 12 points over the state average. This can be seen dramatically in the "improvement flags" display, which shows that Foothills grew in 16 out of 18 possible areas, and passed the highest bar of 6% growth in 5 out of 6 areas, all concentrated among economically disadvantaged and special needs students. Foothills met or exceeded growth targets across the board in science and social studies.

Jarboe noted that these scores would be the envy of many schools across the entire state.

Finally, Foothills grew by nearly 3 points in Readiness and 4.3 points in Graduation Rate.

Such across-the-board achievement needs to be noted and celebrated!
Evaluating Foothills - An Ongoing Study by Richard Welsh

Richard O. Welsh has spent the last year developing an "outside view" study of Foothills. His work continues, but he recently presented a summary of his findings to date to the Foothills Governance Board. His study and presentation focused on four key areas: The Foothills Model, Academic Performance and Students, the Impact of Foothills on Academic Performance and Graduation Rates, and the Impact of Foothills Beyond Test Scores.

Some of Welsh's key findings include:
  • Foothills provides an alternative path for a diverse range of students, particularly those who have previously faced obstacles due to academic obstacles and a variety of personal reasons.
  • Foothills serves a higher percentage of students with disabilities than the overall state average.
  • Foothills uses individualized attention and flexibility to "serve the whole student," and molds the academic experience with out-of-school circumstances in mind.
  • Foothills is a self-reflective and data-driven organization that fosters a culture of continuous improvement.
  • Foothills outperforms the state and partner districts in the area of student growth, maintains an upward trajectory in closing gaps and growth on the CCRPI, performs at the highest levels on the State Charter School Commission Comprehensive Performance Framework, and is one of the top-performing charter schools in state in English Language Arts growth.
  • Perhaps more significant, Foothills continues to provide added value for students, families, and communities beyond that which is measure by test scores alone. Welsh describes the way Foothills is transforming students' life trajectory citing much growth in social and emotional learning. He notes that Foothills students are unanimous in their praise for the support they receive from their teachers.

Of course, there is still work to be done, as all acknowledge. Still, Foothills is worthy of recognition for the work already done, for the more than 1000 graduates to date, and - more globally - for helping to address the nationwide issue of adults 25 and older who still lack a high school diploma.
Note-Taking Initiative

Scott Gordon has recently contacted me and asked me, as part of my Northeast Georgia RESA work with Foothills, to lead a cross-curriculum effort to develop a standard module and protocol to help students with their note-taking skills. The goal is to develop uniform standards and expectations, to help students use the note-taking process to enhance metacognitive learning and to have useful materials for study and review.

This project will roll out over the next few months, starting in January, working with teachers from all content areas. The structure, timing, and use of the newly created module will be developed collectively, and may for example focus on such areas as how to take notes, how to prioritize information, processing and using notes, and reflecting and summarizing.

I am excited (and flattered) to have this opportunity to continue to help Foothills students grow and achieve. Stay tuned for more details in the coming months.
UGA Visit

I was very pleased to have the opportunity to combine two of my "worlds" last month, as I brought my six University of Georgia graduate educational leadership students to the November Curriculum and Data Team meeting. The focus of the course I taught was instructional leadership for student success, and I could think of no better exemplar than the work that has been going on at Foothills for the past four-and-a-half years.

The students were able to sit in on several presentations, including updates on the new Canvas rollout and CCRPI scores. The most impressive part of the evening for them, however, was a face-to-face sit-down meeting with Superintendent Dr. Sherrie Gibney-Sherman, who described how she led the development of Foothills from concept to ongoing success, and the efforts needed to maintain and grow the school.

The students' written feedback revealed just how powerful an experience this was for them. Here are two examples:

Jason Edwards wrote, "During our visit to Foothills, I couldn't help but think about the title of our course, 'Educational Leadership for Student Success.' As leaders, we have no problem placing students who need additional support into appropriate classrooms, but it takes more courage to admit that maybe the traditional school isn't best for the student.

Lawrence Harris added, "Foothills is doing amazing work. I am truly impressed that they have been able to build such a strong team in such a short amount of time.... I can understand that they have many challenges spread out across 16 different sites, but they have built a solid organizational structure from the Superintendent to Regional Directors over very specific operations of the school district.... The fact that all curriculum is developed at the system level and pushed down in a strategic way to the school sites is quite fascinating. They have truly built a scaleable model that is really inspiring and should be considered by other school districts."

If we ever start to take for granted the great successes we are achieving, it is helpful to look at our work from these "outside" eyes.

Many thanks to Dr. Gibney-Sherman, Scott Gordon, and Dr. Mark Tavernier for making my class welcome and for providing such a meaningful learning experience for them.
