Last week our 5 th grade students benefited from a moving and informative gifted field trip from the Museum of Tolerance. Of great interest to me was an explanation of the Four Roles one can play within a problematic social situation:
           Perpetrator: A person who purposefully tries to harm someone else
           Target/Victim: A group or person harmed by the perpetrator
           Bystander: A person who stands by and does not help the target/victim
           Ally: A group or person who helps a target/victim
Our goal, of course, is to put in place a social environment where no individual student acts as a âbullyâ or perpetrator as defined by the Museum of Tolerance. Equally important, however, is to motivate all students to embrace the Ally role. Indeed, in a strong community of Allies, negative behavior is minimized if not eliminated.

Our students heard a powerful Survivorâs story. Born in Amsterdam, Chelly Slagter was left in an institution for Jewish children at age 3, and was transported to a Christian family who raised her as their own. This courageous adoptive family risked their own lives, and in doing so embodied the role of Ally; a powerful story that illustrated the value of an Ally for our students.
Many of our students left the Museum deeply moved.

Our students left the Museum with an Explorer Notebook that contains the Allyâs Pledge:
           I choose to be an ally by speaking out, standing up and getting help.
           I choose to include those who are left out.
           I chose not to be a bully or bystander
           I choose never to keep bullying a secret.
In the coming months, we will roll out a Social Emotional program that supports all of our students to develop effective interpersonal skills. This concept of embracing the roll of Ally will be central to our efforts.

Warmly,
Linda Beck
