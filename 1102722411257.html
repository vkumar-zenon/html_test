Season's Greetings!

In 2020, Poetic Justice Project will enter its twelfth year! In the past eleven years, we've produced Blue Train, Off The Hook, Of Mice and Men, Women Behind the Walls, Planet of Love, The Exonerated, What If?, In the Kitchen with a Knife, Who's Lying Down in Your Heart?, Inside/Out, Wizard of Grey Bar Hotel, Time Will Tell, Blythe and Crossing the Line. How many of these plays have you seen? I'd love to share memories of particular productions in the next PJP update! If you'd like to share a memory, please email it to me at tobola@verizon.net.
 
Part of the joy of working with Poetic Justice Project's actors is witnessing their evolution as actors. But just as rewarding is sharing their journeys as students, workers, family members, leaders and amazing human beings. One of those actors is Caroline Hitch, who talks about her time with Poetic Justice Project.

Hello,
 
I was 19 years old when I first became involved with Poetic Justice Project. I was just a kid. And I felt like an imposter playing the corrections officer in Off The Hook. But I loved being onstage and I loved being a part of something so powerful. Even then I knew I needed PJP, although I hadn't experienced a fraction of what the other cast members had in life. 
 
At 24, I played a trapped prison ghost in In the Kitchen with a Knife. It was a critical time in my life and I was on the verge of getting into trouble one last time. I was saved from that by my character Dodger, the example of my cast mates, the support from Deborah, director Leslie, and the audiences who came to see us. 
 
At 25, I played several characters in the commedia dell'arte production Inside/Out. We wore different masks for each character we played, and in a lot of ways I learned how to take my masks off. It was a small cast and we wrote the play together. We had to work closely and rehearse often. Due to that and the improvisational nature of the play, being able to trust heavily and rely on each other was essential to the smooth running of the show. I learned how to depend on others and become responsible enough for them to depend on me.
 
At 27, I played myself in Time Will Tell. I had to face my fear of vulnerability and share my story with so many people who could judge me. I learned how to step out of my comfort zone, face myself and parts of my past. In turn, I found that connections with others grew deeper and stronger because of it. 
 
Today I'm pushing 30. I'm a pet sitter, I do nature-inspired driftwood art, and work for my partner's plant business. I've been in recovery for more than 6 years. I have a really awesome life. I don't usually feel like an imposter anymore. I know how to work as part of a team, be open about who I am, project my voice, show up on time, depend on others, choose a positive outlet, ask for advice. I've grown up with Poetic Justice Project.
 
Thank you for your support,

Caroline Hitch
