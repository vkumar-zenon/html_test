COMPARISON OF 'WHO PAYS' DATA SHOWS IMPROVEMENT: After five years of raising awareness among shops about the "not-included" repair operations they may be doing regularly - whether or not they are paid for them - the " Who Pays for What? " survey series has found some real-world improvement among the shops that participate in the surveys. Comparing five years of results from the third-quarter survey examining about two dozen "not-included" frame and mechanical operations shows that, on average, 7% more shops are negotiating to be paid for the procedures, and an average of 8.5% more shops are reporting being paid "always" or "most of the time" for them compared to five years ago. Some operations, like draining fuel tanks, have changed little over the five surveys, with one-third of shops still not negotiating for that procedure; about two-thirds of those who do, report regularly being paid for it. But the surveys also have found slow but steady improvement in billing and payment practices for several of the operations. In 2015, 81% of shops said they were not negotiating to be paid for performing destructive test welds; that has dropped dramatically to just 52% this year. Perhaps because performing post-repair test drives is becoming increasingly necessary (and complex) because of ADAS, that is also being added to estimates at a higher rate than five years ago; nearly 1 in 3 shops now report being paid "always" or "most" of the time for such test drives, up from just 13% five years ago. Although roughly the same percentage of shops as five years ago are seeking to be paid for "pre-diagnostic alignment," shops' success in getting paid for it has increased steadily over the five years. "Every shop is different and negotiates differently, but the 'Who Pays' surveys have shown over and over again that shops won't be paid for something they have done if they don't at least know that it is 'not-included,' if they don't determine a fair charge for it, and if they don't put it on their estimates or invoices," trainer and consultant Mike Anderson, who conducts the surveys with CRASH Network, said.
 
 
* SURVEY FINDS FEW WOULD RECOMMEND PARTSTRADER: CRASH Network's recent survey of 244 current users of PartsTrader offered a few nuggets of positive feedback for the electronic parts platform. About two-thirds of shops rated the support and service they receive from PartsTrader as satisfactory, good or excellent; about 60% said the same for the choice of vendors they find on PartsTrader, and a narrow majority rated its "ease of use" as satisfactory or better. Among those who had used PartsTrader for at least two years - almost 3 in 4 had used it for five or more years - 14% felt the system was better or much better than it was two years ago, and only 20% thought it was worse. But those tepidly positive findings came from a survey that also found 78% of users would not recommend PartsTrader to another shop (and another 7% were unsure if they would). On a scale of 1 ("terrible") to 100 ("great"), shops on average gave PartsTrader a rating of 32. Ninety percent said they use it only because an insurance company direct repair program (primarily State Farm's or The Hartford's) requires them to do so, and three-quarters of users said a key reason they wouldn't use it if it weren't required is that "it takes more time or is not as efficient as ordering by other means." "It is ridiculous to explain to an insured that their vehicle is disassembled but waiting for an insurance company mandated program to search for the cheapest part," a shop manager in Michigan said about PartsTrader. In an interview with CRASH Network last week, PartsTrader's Mark Lindner said the survey results do not reflect either the growth he said the company is seeing in its shop customer base, nor the findings of its own internal surveys of customers. Lindner, who joined PartsTrader as "chief revenue officer" this past February (CRASH 6/17/19) , acknowledged the company's platform "absolutely caters to a DRP workflow" but said PartsTrader is "seeing a tremendous increase in independent usage outside of any DRP" requirement. "In fact, one-third of our collision repair facilities that participate on the platform do it independently of any DRP," Lindner said. A survey the company conducted this past June, he said, found that "67% of those surveyed responded that they would recommend PartsTrader." "So we were a little surprised to see the numbers that your survey came back with, because they're quite a bit different from the numbers we're seeing," Lindner said. Watch for more from the CRASH Network survey findings next week; the complete results will be available soon.
 
 
       
* SUBARU SAYS SAFETY INSPECTIONS REQUIRED: Speaking at the Society of Collision Repair Specialists' "OEM Collision Repair Technology Summit" in Las Vegas last month, Nicole Riedel of Subaru of America reiterated the automaker's stance that the post-repair safety inspections called for in its repair procedures must be conducted following any repair of a Subaru (CRASH 9/2/19) . "Every time," Riedel emphasized. "Even if you are in New York City and vehicles are just getting sideview mirrors clipped, you still have to do it [after those repairs]." The procedures can potentially require 15 to 20 labor hours, inspecting wiring and connectors, removing and measuring the steering column, checking seat belt anchors, etc. "We will not deviate from that procedure," Riedel said. "However, we are having conversations with Japan to review that. But at this time, we need you to do it every single time." Riedel said in addition to Subaru's technical information system , shops may find the Subaru owners' website useful for accessing vehicle owner's manuals, as well as its " Service Solutions" website for accessing Subaru-specific tools. She acknowledged that Subaru's "focus on collision is relatively new" so the company welcomes feedback (via email ) on its certified shop network and to improve the thoroughness of its repair procedures. Make any such feedback as specific and detailed as possible, she said. "Don't just say, 'Your repair procedure sucks,'" Riedel joked. "The engineers don't want to hear that. Say something like: 'Here's the business case. Here's how much time I could cut off from this repair. This could keep something from being totaled.' Things like that. Detailed explanations of why we need to look into it."
 
 
* INSURER ADDS GOOGLE REVIEWS TO SHOP LOCATOR: State Farm has added links to Google reviews of the shops on its "Select Service" program to the insurer's online shop locator , perhaps mixing up the dynamics for shops concerned about their ranking on the site. Vehicle owners can use the locator to search for body shops that participate in the State Farm program by city name or zip code, but since 2011, the order of the search results has been based not on proximity but on the shops' performance scores from State Farm (CRASH 10/17/11) . State Farm does not share the formula it uses to establish those scores, which are on a scale from 1 to 1,000, but says scores are updated monthly based on "quality, efficiency and competitive price." Those scores are not shown on the shop locator, but unless a user changes the default settings to search by proximity, the first page of search results will list the three shops within 15 miles that have the highest State Farm score. (Users can then click on "More Shops" to see up to 10 more pages of listings, five shops per page, within the geographic search area, but still ranked by State Farm score, not proximity.) That ranking system has not changed, according to the insurer, but starting this month, under each shop name is a graphical image of up to five stars that Google has assigned the business based on consumer reviews posted to Google. That may give shops with higher Google-star ratings more of a chance to grab the attention of someone using the locator who might otherwise choose a shop on the first page of the search results until they notice those shops have lower (or even just fewer) Google reviews than shops further down the insurer's search results. CRASH Network last week used the locator to search for shops in each state's capital city. State Farm's top-ranked shop in Concord, N.H., for example, had only a 3.3-star rating from Google, based on 35 customer reviews; the shop below it in the search results presumably has a lower State Farm score, but had a 4.8-star Google rating based on 29 customer reviews. Similarly, the first shop in State Farm's ranking for Des Moines, Iowa, had only a 3.7-star rating based on customer reviews, lower than the next 20 shops in the State Farm ranking for that capital city. State Farm's top-ranked shop in five state capitals had Google ratings of 3.9 stars or lower, and a total of 10 had ratings of 4.1 or lower. (The average for the top shop among the state capitals was 4.4 stars.) Those looking for a Select Service shop in Sacramento, Calif., or Baton Rouge, La., might be initially impressed by the 5-star Google rating of the top shop in the search results, but they might also notice the Google scores for both shops are based on only one review each. In the case of the Baton Rouge shop, it's a single, wordless review submitted two years ago by "Hamburger Palmer," and every one of the 31 other Select Service shops shown in the search results for Baton Rouge has more Google reviews. "Online reviews aren't perfect, but at least this gives consumers more information than just how we stack up in terms of State Farm's cost-saving metrics," a "Select Service" shop owner on the West Coast said.
 
 
* INSURERS VOICE CONCERNS ABOUT LARGE SHOP CHAINS: Industry consultant Roger Wright of Vector Squared recently conducted interviews with high-level auto claims executives at eight Top 20 insurance companies (four of the eight were with Top 6 insurers). The interviews, commissioned by Akzo Nobel, started with Wright asking each claims executive what keeps them up at night, followed by an open discussion of whatever topics were of interest to that insurer. Wright said the insurers' concerns could be lumped into eight or nine categories; not surprisingly, "collision repair relationships" was one of those. The insurers generally said they like working with MSOs in that larger repair organizations can offer a single point of contact, may operate call centers, and generally have standard operating procedures in place. Service level agreements with MSOs have become common, with MSOs largely managing their own claims for an insurer based on agreed-upon targets for severity, cycle time, CSI etc. But perhaps surprisingly, Wright said some of the insurers voiced discontent with large MSOs. They have too much staff turnover, some insurers said, and repair quality and customer service can vary too much from location to location. A lack of trust is still prevalent, and insurers said they would prefer not to select an entire MSO as a group but choose among an MSO's individual locations. They actually would prefer to work with single-shop operators or regional MSOs. They also expressed concern something many in the industry have wondered about: That they see the largest MSOs getting big enough to begin leveraging their size to the disadvantage of insurers.

* BACKLOG OF WORK TYPICAL FOR FOURTH QUARTER: The average backlog of scheduled work in shops across the country was nearly flat in October compared to July, increasing by less than half a day on average. It was nearly identical to the fourth quarter backlog measurements in 2018 and 2017 (CRASH 8/26/19) . At the start of the fourth quarter, shops were scheduling 11.7 days into the future on a national average, up from 11.5 days at the beginning of the third quarter. Of the more than 600 shops across the country responding to the survey, just over 30% said they are scheduling work two or more weeks into the future; that's up from the 27.5% last year at this time. As is normal, the single largest group - 36% of shops - say they are scheduling repairs between one and two weeks out, but that's down from 40% of shops at this time last year. About 1 in 5 shops (20%) have less than a week of scheduled work ahead of them. The percentage of facilities nationally that said they are scheduling out four weeks or more rose by about one percentage point this quarter to 6.4%. Looking at the data by region, the Mid-Atlantic region saw a backlog decline of nearly a week since last quarter, dropping to 1.3 weeks (also lower than the 1.6 weeks reported in the region at the start of the fourth quarter a year ago). The Mountain region also was down considerably from last quarter, dropping from 2.6 weeks to 1.8 weeks of average backlog; that's the lowest amount of future work scheduled for that region since the spring of 2018. The Pacific Northwest currently has the longest reported backlog of any region in the contiguous U.S., now an average of 2.3 weeks, up .8 weeks since the start of the third quarter.
