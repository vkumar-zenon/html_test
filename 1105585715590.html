Truss Uplift
Jason R. Gross, E.I.
When the humidity in an attic space gets high enough, truss members will begin to absorb moisture and become saturated. Since the top chord of a truss is exposed it will absorb the moisture and expand, while the bottom chord, buried in insulation, will continue to dry to a low moisture content and shrink. The combination of the top chord expanding and the bottom chord shrinking causes the truss to bow or arch upward. This is referred to as truss uplift.

Truss uplift does not pose a structural threat to the truss or home. However, if the bottom chord of a truss moves upward so will the ceiling material attached to it. This can cause your ceiling to crack or leave gaps between your ceiling and partition walls. A widely used technique to counter the effects of truss uplift is to float the ceiling when it is within 16â of a partition wall (see illustration below). Floating allows for a gradual bend in the drywall, which prevents it from cracking when truss uplift occurs. The drywall clips hold the drywall in place preventing gaps between ceiling and wall.

Several steps can be taken to minimize the amount of truss uplift that occurs. Here are a few of these steps: Make sure that your attic has adequate venting and that the vents are not obstructed. Do not leave trusses exposed to the elements for long periods of time during the construction phase. Use trusses that have been manufactured with high quality lumber such as MSR or MEL. Do not ventilate kitchen and bathroom vents into the attic space.
