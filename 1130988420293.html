Take a Hike, Part Three:
Trail Design
Universal Guidelines
Separate trails from roads.

  • Minimize the number of road crossings, and maintain minimum 5-foot buffers between trails and vehicle travel lanes.
  • Barriers such as bollards or gates control trail access and reduce conflicts with motor vehicles.
  • Street and driveway intersections should incorporate areas for users to wait before crossing.
Fencing or buffer plantings should be utilized to maintain privacy from residential neighbors.
While not every trail has to be ADA-accessible, projects that utilize public money must include accommodations for all users.

Design the corridor for your intended users.
Pedestrians
  • Rural hiking trails should be at least 3 feet wide. Moderate to high volume trails should be at least 5 feet wide to provide comfortable passing room. All trails should have minimum 8-foot-high clearance.
  • Per DCNR, hikers appreciate minimum 2-mile-long trail loops, while joggers prefer minimum 3-mile-long trails.
  • While hiking trails should maintain slopes of less than 18%, interpretive trails and paths intended for universal access should have a maximum 5% slope.
Bicyclists
  • Mountain bike trails should accommodate various skill levels. While single-track trails for advanced riders can be as narrow as 12 inches, 3-foot-wide trails are recommended for general use. High-volume, two-way trails should be at least 6 feet wide. All trails should have 10-foot clearance.
  • Popular local mountain bike trails are at least 9 miles long. Technically challenging trails can be as short as 3 miles.
  • Because users value trails that challenge their abilities, grades can vary from 5% to over 20%. Bypass routes allow beginners to circumvent particularly challenging segments.
  • Cyclists of all levels can utilize paved shared-use paths that are at least 10 feet wide.
Equestrians

  • Single-file trails should be at least 4 feet wide, with minimum 8-foot-wide areas to allow passing. Trails should have minimum 10-foot clearance.
  • A 3- to 5-mile-long trail would provide an hour-long trail ride.
  • Parking for equestrian trails must accommodate trailers. The standard for each trailer parking space is 30 feet wide by 80 feet long. Recommended amenities include mounting blocks, hitching posts, and horse water sources.
Multiple Users

  • By definition, shared use trails should accommodate as many users as possible: joggers, strollers, bicyclists, skaters, and equestrians can all share a properly designed trail.
  • 10 feet is the minimum width for most shared use trails, although trails of up to 14 feet wide are required for high volume trails and multiple user types. 8 feet is the absolute minimum shared use trail width. Overhead clearance should be at least 10 feet.
  • Shared use trails should feature improved surfaces and maximum 5% slopes.
The paved trail at Brightside Farm Park in Charlestown Township is a 1-mile loop. Users can enjoy a quick stroll, or take multiple laps to reach their fitness goals.
Equestrian trails should have good visibility to minimize horses spooking at unfamiliar objects. DCNR recommends maintaining at least 50 feet sight distances, or 100 feet at road crossings.
Shared use trails should incorporate signage to communicate rights-of-way to different users.
Trail Surfaces
Natural Surface Trails

  • Suitable for hiking, nature, mountain bike, and equestrian uses.
  • Erosion should be addressed by routing the trail along, not across, the contours of the land.
Paved Trails

  • Recommended materials for paved trails include compacted gravel, asphalt, and concrete. They provide firm, stable, slip-resistant footing that can accommodate a wider range of users than natural surfaces.
  • Gravel trails are less expensive, but are not suitable for slopes or for areas prone to flooding.
  • While expensive to install, asphalt and concrete are the most durable trail materials, and are more inviting to wheelchair users, bicyclists, and skaters.
This natural surface trail in Charlestown Township is well-used by nearby residents. However, its layout on a steep slope has caused severe erosion issues.
Mulch/wood chip trails require constant maintenance. Wood chips wash away easily, break down quickly, and do not provide firm footing. They are not recommended for trail surfacing.
