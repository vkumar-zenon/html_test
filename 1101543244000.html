Washington Women in Trades is proud to present the 13th and final calendar in our Rosie Legacy Series.


While they began creating calendars in 2007, the Washington Women in Trades Rosie connection began in 2002, when, on a whim, co-chair Robin Murphy, instigated a "Calling all Rosies" gathering at the annual springtime Trades Fair. With the help of some enthusiastic and tenacious media, the dream turned into a luncheon for over 100 Puget Sound women at the Fisher Pavilion at Seattle Center. The attendees were the foremothers of today's tradeswoman, people who had worked in manufacturing during World War II as riveters, mechanics, electricians, machinists, scientists and more. Some even wove giant steel submarine nets!

As time went on and the group formed relationships with the Rosies, they searched for the best way to share the story with the general public. In 2007, they came up with the idea of a calendar, where each year, 12 women would be featured with a photo and short bio. The calendars were as much a success as the original luncheon, and for 13 years, Robin and collaborator, Cindy Payne, found, met, interviewed and photographed new Rosies.

There were in store appearances, signings and panels at The Museum of Flight and MOHAI. For the first time, many of the Roses were being recognized for what they did so very long ago. Most had not considered its importanceâfor them it was simple: they needed work and they took available jobs.. 

But, time marches on and the number of Rosies has dwindled. Most, if they're still with us, are now between 95 and 100 years old. Washington Women in Trades had to face the reality that the Rosies are a national treasure with, unfortunately, an expiration date. Cindy and Robin began searching outside the Puget Sound. Robin attended a National Rosie Convention and met a whole new group of women. She also discovered people in the Centraila/Chehalis area where there had been a small Boeing plant. In 2019, with the help of a Rosie connection in Eugene and an ad in a regional Oregon newspaper, they were able to cobble together one last calendar, The Triumph of Humility. 

The Oregon marathon trip in August of 2019 was the very best way they could have tied the final Rosie ribbon. Against the backdrop of I-5 and some of the most beautiful scenery in the country, they drove from Seattle to Coquille and met nine women in five days. They visited Rosies in their gardens, their living rooms, Elmer's Restaurant and senior centers. They listened to 9 life stories as diverse as meeting celebrities on Rodeo Drive to teaching 2nd grade for over 30 years in a small country school and surviving an Indian boarding school. They met daughters, sons and grandchildren. They took photos and copious notes, then came back to Seattle and created their final opus, the 2020 calendar.

Throughout the years, the Rosies became friends, mentors and confidantes, not just to Payne and Murphy, but to other tradeswomen, too. The Rosies enriched their lives. They invited them into their homes and openly shared their stories--some happy, some funny, some sad and many--very personal. There was laughter, tears and a ton of inspiration. After 13 years, the two had interviewed, photographed and documented the lives of over 150 women!
