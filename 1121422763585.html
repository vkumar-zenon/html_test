Chronicle Of Deregulation/Quadrennial Review (as of 12/5)

December 2018
As anticipated, the FCC voted to begin the 2018 quadrennial review at the December 12th meeting last year.  The Commission began what they called an "open dialogue" for each of the rules it has been instructed to review. Before addressing the potential rules changes though, the FCC must first address the legal challenges to previous reviews.  If we are to see changes, it may not be until next year (2020), and perhaps beyond as 2020 is shaping up to be a big election year. If you are interested in seeing what is set to be discussed, here's a link to the Quadrennial Review Fact Sheet.

FCCAs the FCC's quadrennial review of media ownership rules moves forward, the battle over changes it approved during the previous update to its regulations continues. Public advocacy groups are trying to reverse a decision to lift the cross-ownership ban and the design of the radio incubator program, among other recent changes in regulations. The FCC told the Third Circuit Court of Appeals there's no reason to reverse modernization steps it has already approved. "All of petitioners' challenges to the orders under review are meritless, and their petitions should be denied,".

April 2019
The Quadrennial Review comment window is closed as of an April 29 deadline for the first round of comments in the proceeding. Reply comments were due May 29.  The proposed rulemaking has now been published in the Federal Register.
 
June 2019
On June 11,the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, led by Chief Judge D. Brooks Smith, conducted a hearing that saw a three-judge panel consider opening arguments in  Prometheus Radio Project v. FCC et al. Attorneys for Prometheus and the FCC elaborated on their respective arguments, following a submission to the court of "a very brief summary of the issues in the case."  The Third Circuit gave no hint how soon it will issue a ruling on the latest challenge. Senior FCC officials say that the Commission is unlikely to take any votes on the latest quadrennial review until the appeals court rules on the challenges. Officials say the goal would be for the Commission to use the next court decision to help guide the latest quadrennial proposals. The hope is that would help the FCC when it inevitably ends up back in court defending what are sure to be future legal challenges to the next round of decision-making.

September 2019
On Monday 9/23 a decision was made by the Third Circuit of the U.S. Court of Appeals to vacate and remand the FCC's cross-ownership rule rewrite.  Many in the radio industry are concerned that the Third Circuit decision has put a potentially long pause on any further deregulation. The general consensus is that the chance of ownership sub-caps being lifted is now very low.  At the very least it will likely be held up or delayed for months if not years. With a presidential election in 2020 that could change the attitude of the FCC we could see an administration where media ownership issues remain unresolved and deregulatory relief difficult to achieve.
 
November 2019
The third circuit court rejected the FCC's appeal of media ownership decision.The federal appeals court in Philadelphia has rejected a request for the entire panel to review a decision issued two months ago by a three-judge panel, which blocked several changes to media ownership regulations from remaining on the books. The Federal Communications Commission had sought the review, along with the National Association of Broadcasters and several companies that supported the revisions. They now have the option of filing an appeal with the Supreme Court.  In a brief notice released the court said that the three judges that took part in the original decision didn't request the rehearing and a majority of judges who sit on the court didn't vote in favor of a review. "The petitions for rehearing are denied."  The Third Circuit said the FCC's analysis, which led to the regulatory rollback, was "so insubstantial" that it failed to provide a "reliable foundation" to the court supporting the conclusions. The court told the FCC that it needed to determine what impact the rule changes it proposed would have either through new empirical research or an in-depth theoretical analysis.
