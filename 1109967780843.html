HOW ENGAGED IN COLLEGE CAN YOU EXPECT STUDENTS TO BE?
This month we asked you: " What do you think are realistic expectations for how engaged students will be about college in middle school?" Here's what you said:
 
"Sometimes I think if I knew in middle school what I know now, my college experience would've been very different. I would've focused more on my grades and gotten into a college that provided a very significant scholarship instead of scrimping and graduating with debt. I wish that all middle school students would be engaged so they could learn from my experience, but the truth is that for the great majority, college seems like an eternity away."
 
"I think if we can get just a spark of interest in middle school, that's a huge success. We can't expect middle school students to be as engaged as juniors and seniors in high school. They are busy with their daily lives. To think ahead five years or longer is a lot to ask. I just want to plant the seed that college is something they need to work toward."

"For a sixth grader, the future can seem so very far away. Thinking about graduating from high school and leaving home is just not on their minds. When I'm trying to get my students interested in talking about college and their eyes seem to glaze over, I remind myself that what I'm doing is similar to talking to a kinder about high school. Not only does it seem far away, but there are so many 'adventures' and 'changes' to be had in between. I try to set my own expectations accordingly."
 
"It's surprising to me that there are a few middle school students every year who are already thinking ahead and focused on college. They have an idea of what they may major in and have even figured out some of the colleges they may want to apply to. Many of the rest of the students know that they want to go to college but aren't quite ready to prepare for it. "

College News
A COURSE THAT SHOULD BE OFFERED AT EVERY COLLEGE
There are few if any colleges that offer a course in Building Strategic Relationships, and one expert recommends that this should change. Author David Nour suggests that building strategic relationships can improve both personal and professional relationships and that these skills are not innate to most people. Nour notes that building relationships is about more than handing out business cards and sending thank you notes. Students should have a specific academic or business goal in mind when working on relationships.
HOW MUCH YOU CAN SAVE BY FORGOING DORM LIFE
In many cases, college students can save money by forgoing the dorm. The average annual cost for room and board is $8,990 for community college, $11,510 for public four-year colleges, and $12,990 for private colleges. Multiply this by four, and the costs add up. To save on dorm costs, students may elect to live at home or live off-campus. The exception to this may be colleges in large cities, where the cost of off-campus housing may rival or exceed the cost of the dorm.
COLLEGES GOING BROKE
When it comes to financial news related to colleges, we mostly hear about the rising costs for students and the growth of student loans. However, there's another headline, which is that colleges themselves, especially private liberal colleges in the Northeast and Midwest are experiencing financial challenges. About 20 percent of them are experiencing "fundamental stress" financially according to Moody's due to changes in demographics and the emphasis on technical skills. Unfortunately there aren't many ways for families to determine the financial health of institutions.
WHY STUDENTS DON'T ALWAYS TAKE ADVANTAGE OF SCHOLARSHIP OPPORTUNITIES
Students cite various reasons for not applying for scholarships ranging from lacking the time to find awards to simply not caring. Some students note that applications require a lot of work without a guarantee of success and suggest that their college should do a better job of publicizing awards to students. Others say first-generation students are at a disadvantage because they may not know that scholarship opportunities exist.
