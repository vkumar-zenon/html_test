December 2, 2019

Channel Islands WILD!

A bold new all-encompassing Sierra Club campaign to:

**Protect Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary

** Lead the resistance to stop proposed new offshore oil platforms when the federal government releases plans for increased offshore drilling in federal waters of the Santa Barbara Channel. Plans are scheduled to be released in 2020. Meeting on this issue with the U.S. Secretary of the Interior in January.
** Continue our work for a complete ban on the use of all long-line nylon nets used by the commercial fishing industry to catch a targeted commercial fish species. These same nets lie for miles below the surface of the ocean and entangle and kill hundreds of whales, sea otters, dolphins, seals, diving sea birds, and sea turtles each year.
** Take part in planning sessions and stakeholder meetings for the update to the Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary Management Plan which is ongoing through 2021.
** Continue to work for a northern expansion of the marine sanctuary.
** Lobby members of Congress for passage of legislation which would create several underwater sea mounts, sea caves and sea canyons national marine monuments in the SB Channel. This would prohibit undersea mining.
** Meet with the Japanese Consul General to discourage potential Japanese whaling in federal waters off the coast of SoCal.

**Protect Channel Islands National Park:

** Prevent the removal of Santa Rosa Island from the national park.
** Lobby Congress to move forward with wilderness lands designations as recommended in the Channel Islands National Park General Management Plan, which Congress has never acted upon. (Note: only an act of Congress can create protected federal wilderness areas).
Channel Islands National Park and Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary are special and precious ecosystems which our campaign will aggressively work to protect.

Our Channel Islands WILD ! Team members will fan out across the Sierra Club Los Padres Chapter region of Ventura and Santa Barbara counties, to the state capitol and to the halls of power in Washington, D.C., speaking at events, to groups and organizations, rallying, advocating and meeting with decision-makers to make absolutely certain that all wildlife and wild places in the Channel Islands land and marine ecosystems stay Channel Islands WILD !

Our team is carrying on the Sierra Club tradition of the past 127 years, doing what we do best, protecting the wildlife and wild places which we all love and enjoy.

For Channel Islands WILD!


Jim Hines, Team Leader

October 20, 2019


Greetings Friends:

WE ARE EXCITED! CALIFORNIA WOLF PACK GROWING WITH ARRIVALS FROM OTHER WESTERN STATES!

At our CA Dept. of Fish and Wildlife Wolf Stakeholders meeting this past week, we learned that one of the breeding female gray wolves which is now in the Lassen Pack (northeast CA) actually came to CA from Wyoming. She was tracked by her radio collar.

Until now, all of the wolves entering CA have been from southern Oregon. Several have arrived from Oregon via Nevada. We also have four new wolf pups born this Spring in northeast California.

Our Sierra Club Wildlife Team has two members (appointed by the Governor) on the state wolf stakeholder team.

While the U.S. government is in the process of stripping all wolves in the western U.S. from federal Endangered Species Act protections, wolves in CA will continue to be protected by the California Endangered Species Act.

Our Sierra Club California/Nevada Wildlife Team is part of a national Sierra Club lawsuit to stop the removal of federal protections of wolves in the Western U.S. The current Presidential Administration would like to allow the hunting of wolves in the western U.S.

Wolves, the majestic symbols of the great American wilderness, play a very important part in natural ecosystem balance.

I saw my first wolves in Alaska when I was about 9 year old when I was on a trip with my parents. I have been in awe of wolves ever since. I have seen them on many other trips to Alaska and in Yellowstone National Park.

Our team feels passionately about wolves. We will always attend meetings and hearings advocating and howling for wolves.

For wolves wild and free,

Jim Hines, Team Leader
Sierra Club California/Nevada Wildlife Team


October 26, 2019


Good Morning Friends:

It is a warm beautiful Autumn day on the North Rim of the Grand Canyon. Trees are turning Autumn colors, birds chirp, a deer herd browses. All is serene and quiet, but serene and quiet not for long.

An application has been filed with then U.S. Secretary of the interior to allow for a number of uranium mines to go into production on the north rim of the Grand Canyon this coming Spring, violating one of America's most sacred natural shrines, the Grand Canyon. The stage is set on the saga to save the Grand Canyon from uranium mining, which started four years ago, when President Obama signed an executive order banning any type of mining in and around the Grand Canyon. Last year President Trump signed an order lifting that ban. And now we are before Congress.

We have countered the mine application request with our own application to Congress, which has been turned into a congressional bill (HR 1373, introduced by our good friend Congressman Raul Grijalva D-Arizona) to give permanent protection to the Grand Canyon and not allow the mines. HR 1373 will be voted on by the full House Committee on Natural Resources on October 30h.

Please contact the House Committee on Natural Resources:
Access by phone (202) 225-6065
Access by fax: (202) 225-5929

Ask committee members to vote YES on HR 1373. Sharing any of your personal experiences about visits to the Grand Canyon, and what would be lost to YOU if uranium mining is allowed, is the most positive way to express yourself. I have learned this from my many meeting with members of Congress, both Democratic and Republican

Once you destroy the Grand Canyon, you cannot just go down to the local home improvement store, and buy some bricks ad mortar and put the Grand Canyon all back together.

#keepitgrand

For a Grant Canyon beautiful and majestic forever,

Jim

October 28, 2019


Good Morning Friends:

Almost out of breath, a small red fox is running rapidly through a meadow headed to her den, where she left her pups. But now far behind her is an employee of the U.S. Dept. of Agriculture's Dept of Wildlife Services (DWS).

The DWS employee is not headed towards the fox den to study the fox. He is there to place a "cyanimide bomb" to kill all the foxes in the den.

Why? Because someone reported to DWs that a fox was around his/her chicken pen; they called DWS whose job it is to kill any wildlife committing a wildlife crime (i.e., being in the wrong place at the wrong time). Example: a person sees one fox near a chicken pen. He/she calls DWS, who kills ALL foxes in the general area.

In 2018 alone, 30,000 foxes, bears, wolves, mountain lions, beavers, and even birds of prey have been killed in California; another 60,000 have been killed in Nevada. This happens all across the West.

This is a U.S.Government War on Wildlife.

So this week my wildlife team is headed to meetings with DWS staff in Sacramento, CA and in Reno, Nevada. The DWS should only use lethal methods as a last resort, but in reality DWS uses lethal killing (poisoning, bombing, shooting, and leg traps) as a first resort, and most of the time lethal killings are all that are used.

We have hopes that our meetings this week with DWS staff will generate interest in using non-lethal methods. If not, we are prepared to petition Congress to enact legislation to mandate the DWS to use non-lethal wildlife control methods as a priority of the DWS.

As one retired DWS employee told me: "My job was to kill wildlife, kill wildlife, kill wildlife, and kill more wildlife."

We feel the USDA's Dept. of Wildlife Services is out of control and needs strong congressional oversight.

For wildlife,

Jim Hines, Team Leader
sierra Club California/Nevada Wildlife Team

November 1, 2019

Good Morning Friends:

The ocean waters surrounding Channel Island National Park are teeming with breaching whales, playful dolphins, frolicking sea otters, and magnificent giant sea turtles. Thousands of people go on whale watching trips each year and scientists from around the world study and explore the waters of Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary.

But all this is about to change, and not for the better. Threats are now emerging which will destroy the spectacular marine mammals which we all love. All isn't well in the political world when it comes to protecting our ocean environment and the magnificent creatures who inhabit the marine ecosystem.

We will go off to meetings this coming week with National Marine Fisheries officials who are proposing to allow deadly long-line drift nets in federal waters off the coast of California. These are the deadly nylon lines which lie in the ocean for miles to catch a targeted commercial fish species, but also to entangle and kill whales, sea lions, sea otters, diving sea birds, dolphins, and sea turtles by the hundreds each year.

This is a complete reversal from past discussions where the U.S. Government had pledged to work with us to ban all longline nets (the state of California has banned these nets in state ocean waters).

We will also be discussing a proposal to create a protected Humpback Whale Sanctuary in federal waters in the Santa Barbara Channel off our coast and the opposition of the Trump Administration to that designation.

Then on to a meeting with our friend U.S. Senator Diane Feinstein regarding her bill to ban all new offshore oil drilling in federal waters off the coast of California.

Our agenda is also the work going on behind the scenes in Washington, D.C., on plans to roll back or perhaps eliminate the Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary. Local sanctuary management staff are doing an outstanding job of protecting all marine life with the Sanctuary, but those who oversee the system in Washington D.C. (the U.S. Secretary of Commerce) have plans to reduce/ eliminate America's national marine sanctuary system. Allowing for unlimited offshore oil drilling operations, which fits into the plans of the Trump Administration to drill on ALL federal lands and waters, the proposal talked about for the waters offshore of Ventura and Santa Barbara counties is around 70 new drilling platforms. We are awaiting an answer to our request to meet with the U.S. Secretary of Commerce.

For an ocean where marine mammals can swim wild and free,

Jim Hines, Team Leader
Sierra Club California/Nevada Wildlife Team



National Parks Threatened with Closure

November 5, 2019

Good morning Friends:

Here we go again. The federal government may shut down America's national parks, national monuments, and other national public lands units on November 21, 2019. Guess who cannot visit YOUR national parks? Yep, that's right. YOU.

But there will be a loophole in the national park closure--if you own or operate an oil and gas drilling company or a mining company, you can go right in and set up shop and keep drilling and mining in America's national parks and other national public lands while these same parks are closed to the public. A similar loophole was allowed during the last national park closure in 2018.

White House staff has told top Dept. of the Interior officials that the President may have no choice but to shut down the U.S. Government this month because of the impeachment process going on in Congress right now. Staff say that the upcoming U.S. government national public lands closure should not be allowed to affect what makes America great: oil and gas drilling and mining in national parks, national monuments, and other national public lands units.

There will be more in-depth discussion on national park closure plans at our Dept. of the Interior call next week.

So be prepared. Do not plan any visits to enjoy the beautiful Autumn season in America's national parks unless you have an oil rig or mining tractor.

For the wild,

Jim

WILDLIFE AND LAND CONSERVATION UPDATES

Gray Wolf Status (October 2019)
We are winning the struggle to protect one of North America's great wildlife species: wolves. The Sierra Club Wildlife Team is happy to report that the Lassen Pack (Northern California) is now up to seven members, with the addition of three pups born this summer.

Two years ago then-Governor Brown appointed one of our wildlife team members to be on the CA Dept of Fish and Wildlife Wolf Stakeholder Team. Two months ago CA Governor Newsom appointed a second member of our Wildlife Team to work with the State to make sure wolves and their habitat in CA are protected.

The Wildlife Protection Act of 2019 Now Law
On September 4, 2019, Governor Gavin Newsom signed into law The Wildlife Protection Act of 2019. California thus becomes the first state in the nation to ban fur trapping. The Wildlife Protection Act of 2019 bans commercial and recreational trapping of animals for their fur on both public and private lands.

Update on HR 871.

HR 871 has been referred to the House Committee on Natural Resources. We will keep our readers informed of its progress.

Update on AB 1788. Banning Rodenticides in CA
Action on AB1788, which bans the sale and use of deadly anti-coagulant rodenticides, has been postponed until the 2020 State Legislative session.

Melba Angeline Simms
In Memoriam
1928-2019
Melba Angeline Simms, dedicated wife, mother, peace activist and conservationist, died at home Sept. 28, 2019, at age 90. Melba was born in Los Angeles on November 4, 1928. She graduated from Huntingon Park High School in 1946 and found a secretarial job with the IRS in downtown Los Angeles. In 1953, she married Willis Simms and moved into the house he built in Beverly Glen. They raised two daughters, Ellen Louise and Laura E liza beth, in the San Fernando Valley.

Melba enjoyed travelling. She and Willis camped in many of the parks of California, travelled through Europe twice, and drove to both Oaxaca and Vancouver with their daughters. After their children were grown, they backpacked, visited Europe again, and took a cruise to view a solar eclipse.

At home, Melba maintained an extensive garden in her backyard, volunteered in the schools, and enjoyed reading with children through Grandparents and Books at the West Valley Branch of the LA Public Library.
She and Willis were active in the SANE/Nuclear Freeze Movement and the Santa Monica Mountains Trust. They were also deeply involved in the activities of the San Fernando Valley Group of the Angeles Chapter of the Sierra Club. Both Melba and Willis often led Sierra Club hikes in the Santa Monica Mountains, which they were determined to protect from development. For many years, Melba and Willis produced, published and distributed the Sundays in the Santa Monicas hike schedule.

Melba will be sorely missed by her daughters and sons-in-law, Ellen L. Simms and Thomas Colton of Berkeley, CA, and Laura E. Simms and William Capman of Minneapolis, MN. Two granddaughters also survive her--Nyssa and Emma Capman. She was preceded in death by her husband, her parents, her sister and her brother. Her later years were made more joyful by her loving relationship with Sansar Singh Gill, who survives her.

A celebration of Melba's life was held at Emerson Unitarian Church on October 26th, 2019. The family asks that donations be made in her memory to the Sierra Club, KPFK Radio, or the Los Angeles Public Library.

Fanged Mouse Deer Discovered in Vietnam Forest


A tiny species of wildlife, which greatly resembles a miniature deer and which has fangs, has been discovered in the forests of southern Vietnam.

The last time this animal had been seen was 30 years ago, when a hunter killed one and donated the body to a team of Russian and Vietnamese scientists for study.

The animal, known formally as the Silver-Backed Chevrotain, had been thought extinct. It is approximately the size of a large rabbit, with a silver sheen to the fur on its rump. This creature has tusk-like incisors and miniature hooves.

In spite of its nickname, the Chevrotain is neither mouse nor deer. It is the world's smallest ungulate (hoofed mammal), according to Global Wildlife Conservation. Chrevrotains are shy and very solitary. They usually to walk on the tips of their tiny hooves. Each animal has two miniature fangs. The weight of these miniscule animals is less than 10 lbs.

Chevrotains forage for fruits and plants during the day.

Andrew Tilker, a biologist specializing in Southeast Asian wildlife at Global Wildlife Conservation, an environmental group, says that the species was first described by scientists in 1910 , when four Chevrotains were collected near Nha Trang. There were no more verifiable records again until 1990 , when one animal was taken from a local hunter and donated to researchers.

Tilker stated that the GWC wanted to search for the remaining species, but didn't know where to begin. "The species was one giant question mark," he remarked. They knew that formerly people had camera-trapped the wet evergreen forests and had not seen the Chevrotain, so they decided to search the dry forest habitat, which is further north. There they were successful.

Chevrotains are a highly endangered species. Because bushmeat is so highly prized in East Asia, these animals are illegally hunted. Poachers set wire snare traps which can catch and kill any wildlife that walks on the forest floor.

"Discovering that the Chevrotains are indeed still out there, our first step is to ensure we don't lose them again. Now that we have re-found these tiny deer-like creatures, we are moving quickly to figure out how to best protect them," vowed Vietnamese biologist An Nguyen of the GWC.
